Mission & Vision

Who We Are &
What We Stand For

"To empower young Zimbabweans in the UK to Revisit their heritage, regrow their cultural identity, and embrace the richness of who they are."

Our vision is a generation of confident, rooted, and faith-filled young Zimbabweans in the UK who carry their cultural identity with pride, transmit it to the next generation, and contribute meaningfully to both the Zimbabwean community and the wider society they live in.

COHOPE Culture School operates at the intersection of cultural restoration, personal identity, and community renewal — equipping young people to thrive in both their heritage and their home.

Ubuntu / Unhu
I am because we are. Every activity and decision is shaped by communal belonging — not individual achievement alone.
Respect
Ukuhlonipha / Kuremekedza — respect for elders, peers, self, and God underpins every session within COHOPE.
Honesty
We engage truthfully with hard questions — about gender, colonisation, faith, and culture — without pretence or avoidance.
Faith
Our work is rooted in the Anglican Christian tradition. Scripture is the lens through which we examine culture.
Inclusivity
Both Shona and Ndebele heritage are honoured equally. The programme belongs to all Zimbabweans.
Empowerment
Participants leave every session with more — more knowledge, more confidence, more tools — not simply more information.
Three Pillars of Purpose

The Framework
Behind Everything

Three words. One complete framework for cultural restoration, personal identity, and community renewal.

01 Revisit Look Back with Honesty

Understand where we come from — our history, our customs, our proverbs, our family structures. Explore the great kingdoms of Zimbabwe: Great Zimbabwe, the Rozvi Empire, the Munhumutapa Kingdom. Know your totem. Know your clan. Look back without shame, and find your roots.

02 Regrow Restore from the Roots

Restore what has been lost through self-colonisation and the pressure to assimilate. Rebuild cultural confidence and identity from the ground up. Replace the internalised belief that our culture is inferior with pride, curiosity, and ownership. Regrow what belongs to you.

03 Embrace Live Fully, Carry Forward

Live fully as a Zimbabwean and a Christian in the UK. Carry heritage forward with joy — in the home, the church, and the community. You do not have to choose between your heritage and your home. Embrace both. Transmit both. Be both, completely.

Programme Objectives

Eight Objectives.
One Complete Vision.

Together these form a whole — a complete framework for cultural restoration, personal identity, and community renewal.

8
Objective 01

Restore Cultural Identity

Reconnect young Zimbabweans with their heritage and help them understand who they are — beyond skin colour, accent, or food.

  • Articulate Zimbabwean identity with confidence and depth
  • Explore pre-colonial history, kingdoms and civilisations
  • Understand totems (mitupo/izibongo) and clan structures
  • Address the concept of self-colonisation and replace it with pride
Objective 02

Preserve Shona & Ndebele Languages

Ensure that Shona and Ndebele do not die in the diaspora — and that young people have the tools and confidence to speak and pass on these languages.

  • Build conversational confidence in greetings, proverbs, and family terms
  • Explore the deeper cultural meaning carried within the language
  • Inspire creative expression through spoken word, song, and storytelling
Objective 03

Educate on Family Structures & Traditions

Provide accurate, nuanced, and honest education on roora, marriage, gender roles, family hierarchy, and the Ubuntu philosophy of communal life.

  • Demystify roora — its original spiritual, relational, and covenantal purpose
  • Create space for honest conversation on gender roles and dignity
  • Help participants navigate the gap between Zimbabwean and UK cultural norms
Objective 04

Bridge Zimbabwean Culture & Christian Faith

Demonstrate that Zimbabwean cultural heritage and Anglican Christian faith are not in opposition — and that much of our culture finds its deepest meaning in Scripture.

  • Draw connections between Zimbabwean customs and Old Testament covenant practices
  • Enable participants to be fully Zimbabwean and fully Christian
  • Encourage culturally integrated worship: Shona and Ndebele praise within Anglican liturgy
Objective 05

Celebrate Zimbabwean Arts & Creative Life

Ensure that the arts — mbira, hosho, traditional song, visual art, storytelling — remain living expressions of Zimbabwean identity, not museum pieces.

  • Hands-on engagement with traditional Zimbabwean instruments and music
  • Introduce stone sculpture traditions, Great Zimbabwe motifs, beadwork, pottery
  • Build an archive of creative work — a living cultural record of the diaspora generation
Objective 06

Build Intergenerational Community

Create meaningful bonds between elders, parents, young adults, and teenagers — so that cultural knowledge flows naturally from one generation to the next.

  • Bring elders into the programme as honoured teachers and cultural custodians
  • Establish peer mentoring: 25–30s mentoring 18–24s; 18–24s mentoring 15–17s
  • Help parents understand UK-raised children with empathy and without dismissal
Objective 07

Develop the Next Generation of Leaders

Identify, equip, and commission young Zimbabweans who will carry COHOPE forward and take up leadership roles in their church, community, and professional sphere.

  • Introduce servant leadership through a Zimbabwean and Christian lens
  • Develop personal vision statements: who am I? what do I carry? how will I lead?
  • Commission participants formally as cultural custodians and community builders
Objective 08

Support Mental Wellbeing & Belonging

Address the hidden cost of cultural displacement — the sense of not belonging anywhere — and create a community where young Zimbabweans are fully seen and fully welcomed.

  • Acknowledge the psychological impact of living between two cultures
  • Address identity-related shame, imposter syndrome, and cultural embarrassment
  • Equip participants with language to navigate race, identity, and belonging in the UK
Programme Structure

Five Pillars of
the Programme

Each pillar targets a specific dimension of the challenge facing young Zimbabweans in the UK — with clear objectives and measurable outcomes.

Programme Pillar Key Objective Expected Outcome
Identity & Heritage
 Restore knowledge of pre-colonial Zimbabwe, totems, clans, and the meaning of cultural identity in a diaspora context Participant can articulate their Zimbabwean identity, knows their totem, and has connected with at least one piece of their family history
Family, Relationships & Roora
 Educate on roora, Ubuntu, gender, and family structures with honesty and without reducing complex traditions to stereotypes Participant understands the true meaning of roora and can discuss Zimbabwean family culture with confidence and nuance
Language, Arts & Creative Expression
 Build language confidence and creative identity through Shona/Ndebele, music, visual art, and spoken word Participant has performed creatively in or about their heritage and commits to using their language more intentionally
Faith, Culture & Anglican Tradition
 Demonstrate the convergence of Zimbabwean cultural wisdom and Scripture; equip participants for integrated faith and identity Participant can identify biblical parallels to Zimbabwean custom and worships without internal cultural conflict
Community, Leadership & Future Builders
 Develop leaders, equip parents, and build a thriving intergenerational community of culturally confident Zimbabweans Participant has a personal vision statement and is connected to a network of like-minded young Zimbabweans
Who This Programme Is For

A Programme Built
for Every Young Zimbabwean

Wherever you are in your cultural journey — whether you speak fluent Shona or have never heard your totem — there is a place for you here.

Ages 15 – 18
Teens

Cultural identity formation at the most critical stage of development. Building pride before assimilation pressures take full hold. Creating peer community with shared heritage.

Ages 19 – 24
Young Adults

Navigating university, work, and relationships across cultural borders. Understanding roora, relationships, and identity as independent adults. Building a network of peers who share their experience.

Ages 25 – 30
Senior Youth

Entering or already in parenthood. Needing tools to transmit culture to the next generation. Ready to take on leadership and mentoring roles within the community.

UK-Born
UK-Born Participants

May have little Shona or Ndebele and feel disconnected from Zimbabwean heritage. COHOPE provides access without judgement — a beginning, not an examination.

Zimbabwe-Born
Zimbabwe-Born Participants

May feel caught between two identities. COHOPE validates both their heritage and their UK experience — they do not have to choose.

Parenting Stage
Young Parents

Specifically supported through Pillar 5. Given practical tools to raise culturally rooted children in a UK context. The next generation starts here.

Measuring Success

How We Know
It's Working

COHOPE Culture School is committed to honest evaluation at every level — from individual participants to the whole community.

Participant-Level Indicators
Pre- and post-session feedback measuring change in confidence, knowledge, and identity
Completion of take-home challenges — calling a family elder, speaking Shona/Ndebele at home, completing a family tree
Personal vision statement produced by each participant by end of the programme
Number of participants who take on volunteer or leadership roles within COHOPE
Self-reported sense of belonging and cultural confidence at programme end
Programme-Level Indicators
Attendance rates across all 10 sessions (target: 70% attend 6+ sessions)
Number of elders, parents, and guest speakers engaged across the programme year
Quality and breadth of creative outputs: spoken word, art, language recordings, family tree projects
Number of participants progressing to co-facilitate or lead future sessions
Annual Culture Day showcase attendance and participant contributions
Community-Level Indicators
Intergenerational connections formed — measured by participant self-report and elder feedback
Number of families reporting cultural transmission activities at home following the programme
Engagement from the wider Anglican church community in Zimbabwean cultural events
Year-on-year growth in programme enrolment and community reach

"We measure success not only by numbers but by stories. A teenager who calls their grandmother in Zimbabwe for the first time to ask about their totem. A young father who starts speaking Shona to his children. A young woman who stands before her church and reads Scripture in Ndebele. These are the outcomes that cannot be counted — and they are the ones that matter most."

Our Faith Framework

Faith Is Not an
Add-On. It Is the Foundation.

COHOPE Culture School operates explicitly within the Anglican Christian tradition. We do not syncretise. We illuminate.

Our Theological Position

While Zimbabwean culture has deep roots in vadzimu traditions, most cultural practices and values find clear parallels in the Old Testament. We hold that God was not absent from Zimbabwe before the missionaries arrived — and that our culture, rightly understood, often reflects the same divine order encoded in Scripture.

✦
Every session includes a Scripture connection — a biblical passage that illuminates the cultural theme being explored
✦
Opening and closing prayers anchor each workshop in faith, often delivered in Shona or Ndebele
✦
The Church Patron (Anglican clergy) provides theological oversight and pastoral support throughout
✦
Complex areas — vadzimu, traditional ceremonies, gender — are engaged honestly from a Christ-centred position
✦
Participants are never required to leave their culture at the door of the church, nor their faith at the door of the culture

"Tinohakira nezvisiri zvedu" — no more.

We will graze no longer on what is not ours.
